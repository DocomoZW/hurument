# Executive Briefing
## Governance and Risk Management in Zimbabwe: State of Play and Strategic Priorities

*Prepared for senior leadership and decision-makers | May 2026*
*Based on: NDS2 (2026–2030) | zimGov institutional analysis | GRC procurement intelligence (eGP portal)*

---

## The Bottom Line

Zimbabwe's public sector governance transformation is real and accelerating — but the gap between policy ambition and institutional performance remains the defining risk to Vision 2030. The frameworks are in place. The question is execution.

---

## Where Zimbabwe Stands

Zimbabwe is mid-transition from a manually managed, compliance-reactive public sector to a digitally-enabled, risk-proactive governance architecture. Three forces are driving this shift simultaneously:

**1. Strategic direction from the top.** NDS2 (2026–2030) elevates governance, institutional integrity, and risk management to foundational national priorities — not just thematic areas. Cabinet Coordinating Committees chaired at Vice President level now oversee ten national priority working groups. The Citizen Satisfaction Index target of 70% by 2030 creates a measurable accountability floor.

**2. Operational digitalization on the ground.** The electronic Government Procurement (eGP) portal has become Zimbabwe's most visible anti-corruption infrastructure. Public entities are actively procuring Enterprise Risk Management systems, COBIT-based IT governance frameworks, real-time risk dashboards, and automated whistleblower platforms. This is not aspirational — these systems are live and in use.

**3. External accountability pressure.** Zimbabwe's 2024 accession to the African Peer Review Mechanism (APRM) and its first peer review on Economic Governance in 2025 have introduced externally validated, publicly visible governance assessment cycles. The pressure to perform is no longer only internal.

---

## Three Things That Are Working

**Enterprise Risk Management is taking institutional root.** The 2023 Public Sector Risk Framework — mandating Risk Appetite Statements, centralized Risk Registers, and ERM training across all MDAs — is generating measurable procurement demand for integrated risk management systems. The National Building Society's deployment of a consolidated Audit, Compliance, and Operational Risk platform replacing manual spreadsheets is a marker of genuine adoption.

**Procurement integrity is structurally enforced.** The eGP portal's administrative compliance requirements — NSSA certificates, ZIMRA clearances, PRAZ registration — function as automatic gatekeeping mechanisms. No document, no contract, regardless of technical merit. This is a structural anti-corruption control that works at scale.

**Anti-corruption institutions are expanding.** ZACC now has provincial offices in Mashonaland West and Central. Over 1,100 integrity pledges have been signed with public officials. Specialized anti-corruption courts are operational. The institutional infrastructure for accountability exists. The variable is will and consistency of application.

---

## Three Things That Are Not Working

**Devolution funds are not reaching sub-national tiers on time.** The Constitution mandates 5% of national revenue to provincial and local authorities. NDS1 consistently fell short. Delayed disbursements stall infrastructure projects, undermine local governance capacity, and erode public trust. This is the single most corrosive implementation failure.

**Inter-agency coordination remains weak.** NDS1 thematic working groups frequently lacked senior-level chairing, resulting in duplicated efforts, wasted resources, and accountability gaps. NDS2's structural fix — Vice President-chaired committees — is a design improvement, not a behavioral guarantee. The risk of coordination failure persists.

**Whistle-blower protection legislation does not exist.** Platform-based whistleblower systems are being deployed (TIMB's Tip-Off Anonymous is operational). But without legislation, reporters have no legal protection from retaliation. This is a material gap in Zimbabwe's anti-corruption architecture. Legislation is promised under NDS2. Every year it is delayed weakens the system.

## The Five Risks That Demand Attention

| Risk | Category | Consequence if unaddressed |
|---|---|---|
| Inflation above single-digit target | Macroeconomic | ZiG instability, capital flight, erodes real value of public investment |
| Cyber-attack on critical public infrastructure | Digital / Operational | Service disruption, data breach, loss of public trust |
| Climate shock overwhelming NEOC capacity | Disaster / Climate | Humanitarian crisis, infrastructure loss, fiscal strain |
| SOE underperformance under Mutapa oversight | Governance / Financial | Contingent liability crystallization, fiscal pressure |
| IRBM monitoring system non-operationalization | Institutional | Inability to track NDS2 progress; accountability vacuum |

---

## What Needs to Happen in the Next 18 Months

**1. Operationalize the IRBM performance system.** The e-Whole of Government Performance Management System must be functional and reporting by end of 2026. Without it, NDS2 monitoring is manual, lagging, and unreliable.

**2. Enact whistle-blower legislation.** Draft, consult, and pass the Whistle-blower Protection Act. This is a short legislative intervention with outsized governance impact.

**3. Enforce the 5% IGFT disbursement.** Ring-fence devolution transfers from Treasury so they are automatic, not discretionary. Publish quarterly disbursement data publicly.

**4. Extend COBIT/cybersecurity governance to all critical systems.** ZIMDEF's COBIT 2019 deployment is a template. Roll out to all entities managing citizen data, financial systems, or national infrastructure within 24 months.

**5. Publish the 2025 APRM findings with a government response.** Transparency on the peer review outcomes — including areas needing improvement — converts the APRM from a periodic exercise into a continuous accountability mechanism.

---

## The Governance Investment Case

For development partners, investors, and policymakers, the core question is whether Zimbabwe's governance transformation is durable or cyclical. The evidence supports durability — with conditions.

**The case for durability:** Constitutional anchoring, the PPDPA Act, the eGP system, and the 2023 ERM framework are structural, not personality-dependent. The APRM creates external validation cycles. The Mutapa Fund's Santiago Principles alignment signals international accountability norms.

**The conditions:** Sustained political will to enforce compliance at the board level; budget discipline that closes the commitment-disbursement gap; and consistent, non-selective anti-corruption prosecution.

Zimbabwe is building the right architecture. The test of this period is whether institutions learn to use it under pressure.
