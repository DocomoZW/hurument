## Institutional Frameworks, Digital Transformation, and the Path to Vision 2030

**A Whitepaper**

## Executive Summary

Zimbabwe is undergoing a decisive shift in how the state governs itself, manages public risk, and holds institutions accountable. Driven by constitutional imperatives, the National Development Strategy 2 (NDS2, 2026–2030), and a rapidly maturing public procurement ecosystem, the country is transitioning from fragmented, manually-managed compliance processes to an integrated, digitally-enabled Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) architecture.

This whitepaper synthesizes findings across three domains: the operational GRC landscape as revealed through Zimbabwe's electronic Government Procurement (eGP) system; the institutional governance framework anchored in the Office of the President and Cabinet (OPC); and the strategic risk management provisions of NDS2. Together, these reveal a coherent, if still-evolving, national governance transformation story.

**Key findings:**

- Zimbabwe's public sector is institutionalizing Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) across all Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs), guided by the 2023 Public Sector Risk Framework.
- Digital GRC tools — including real-time risk dashboards, COBIT 2019-based IT governance systems, automated whistleblower platforms, and ISO 9001-certified quality management systems — are being actively procured and deployed.
- NDS2 elevates governance to a foundational national priority, targeting a Citizen Satisfaction Index of 70% by 2030 and embedding anti-corruption as a "whole-of-government" imperative.
- Systemic challenges persist: funding constraints, weak inter-agency coordination, and administrative non-compliance continue to hamper implementation.
- A coherent path forward exists, anchored on the Integrated Results-Based Management (IRBM) framework, the African Peer Review Mechanism (APRM), and the Mutapa Investment Fund's SOE oversight architecture.

## 1. Introduction

Good governance is not an administrative nicety — it is the precondition for economic transformation. For Zimbabwe, pursuing its Vision 2030 ambition of becoming a Prosperous and Empowered Upper Middle-Income Society, governance reform and systematic risk management are not peripheral concerns; they are load-bearing pillars.

Zimbabwe's governance transformation unfolds on three interconnected levels. The constitutional and institutional level establishes who governs, under what authority, and through what oversight mechanisms. The strategic level — expressed through NDS2 — defines what the government is trying to achieve and the risk landscape that could prevent it. The operational level — visible in the procurement decisions of public entities on the eGP portal — reveals how governance principles are being translated into day-to-day institutional practice.

This whitepaper draws on evidence from all three levels to provide an integrated analysis of Zimbabwe's current governance and risk management posture, identify structural strengths and gaps, and offer actionable recommendations for practitioners, policymakers, and development partners.

## 2. Zimbabwe's Constitutional and Institutional Governance Architecture

### 2.1 Constitutional Foundations

Zimbabwe's governance framework is anchored in the supremacy of the Constitution, which establishes the rule of law, fundamental human rights, gender equality, and good governance as founding values. Section 3 of the Constitution binds the state and all institutions at every level of government to these principles.

This constitutional architecture creates the legal foundation for the governance transformation currently underway. Key legislation operationalizing these constitutional principles includes:

- **Public Entities Corporate Governance Act [Chapter 10:31]** — Mandates corporate governance standards for public entities and state-owned enterprises.
- **Public Procurement and Disposal of Public Assets (PPDPA) Act [Chapter 22:23]** — Institutionalizes transparent procurement, currently operationalized through the eGP portal.
- **Cyber and Data Protection Act [Chapter 12:07]** — Establishes the legal framework for cybersecurity governance and data privacy across all public institutions.

### 2.2 The Three-Tier Governance Structure

Zimbabwe operates through three interrelated tiers of government: national, provincial/metropolitan, and local authority. Chapter 14 of the Constitution provides the framework for devolution and decentralization, which NDS2 has elevated to a central governance pillar.

The **Office of the President and Cabinet (OPC)** serves as the apex coordinating body, providing overall policy direction, ensuring role clarity across all agencies, and facilitating the appointment of ministers, commissioners, ambassadors, and state corporation boards. Cabinet exercises strategic oversight supported by Cabinet Coordinating Committees — chaired at Vice President level — and ten Thematic Working Groups aligned to NDS2 priority areas.

Devolution targets the transfer of meaningful power and resources to sub-national tiers. The Constitution mandates an Inter-Governmental Fiscal Transfer of at least **5% of national revenue** to provincial and local authorities annually — a commitment that fell short during NDS1, with disbursements consistently below target.

### 2.3 Oversight Institutions

Zimbabwe's governance system is reinforced by a network of specialized constitutional and statutory bodies:

| Institution | Mandate |
|---|---|
| Zimbabwe Anti-Corruption Commission (ZACC) | Investigate and combat corruption across government |
| Office of the Auditor General | Financial oversight and audit of public accounts |
| Zimbabwe Human Rights Commission (ZHRC) | Protect constitutional rights |
| Zimbabwe Electoral Commission (ZEC) | Electoral governance |
| Public Service Commission | Civil service standards and appointments |
| Procurement Regulatory Authority of Zimbabwe (PRAZ) | Procurement oversight and eGP system management |
| National Prosecuting Authority | Prosecution of criminal matters including corruption |

NDS2 commits to empowering all governance oversight institutions to fully exercise their functions over MDAs, state-owned enterprises, and public officials — a recognition that institutional capacity, not merely legal authority, is the binding constraint.

## 3. Strategic Direction: NDS2 and the Governance Agenda

### 3.1 Governance as a Foundational Pillar

NDS2 (2026–2030) positions governance, institution building, peace, and security as "a foundational pillar essential for fostering stability, integrity, and sustainable development." This framing represents a deliberate elevation from NDS1, where governance was one of fourteen thematic areas. Under NDS2, it is architecturally prior to all other priorities: economic transformation, infrastructure, and social service delivery all depend on functional governance institutions.

The strategy's governance commitments encompass:

- **Transparency and accountability** — zero tolerance for corruption across public and private sectors
- **Service delivery standards** — all public institutions required to develop standards aligned with regional and global best practices, with a Citizen Satisfaction Index target of 70% by 2030
- **Whistle-blower legislation** — enactment of protections for those reporting corruption, recognized as critical for building an accountability culture
- **Asset declaration** — mandatory declaration of assets by all public officials, via a proposed amendment to the Public Service Act
- **Anti-corruption courts** — strengthening of specialized courts to adjudicate corruption cases efficiently

### 3.2 Integrated Results-Based Management (IRBM)

NDS2 anchors implementation on the **Integrated Results-Based Management (IRBM)** framework, which integrates planning, budgeting, human resource management, information systems, and performance monitoring into a unified management architecture. This is not merely a planning methodology — it is an accountability instrument.

Under IRBM:
- All MDAs operate under **Performance Contracts** with annual strategic plan validations overseen by the OPC.
- An **e-Whole of Government Performance Management System** enables real-time tracking of key performance indicators and automated data analysis.
- **Data Units** will be established in all MDAs, capacitated by ZimStats, to ensure systematic data collection, analysis, and reporting.
- Quarterly and annual reporting cycles feed into evidence-based policy adjustments.

This framework addresses a critical lesson from NDS1: weak monitoring and evaluation systems made it difficult to track progress, ensure accountability, or make timely adjustments.

### 3.3 Devolution and Decentralization

Devolution under NDS2 moves from aspiration to implementation. The strategy commits to:
- Timely disbursement of devolution funds to support transformative infrastructure programs at provincial and local levels.
- Devolving governmental powers and responsibilities to Provincial and Metropolitan Councils and local authorities.
- Decentralizing justice delivery, civil registration, and government services to bring them closer to communities.
- Fiscal devolution measured against the constitutional 5% Inter-Governmental Fiscal Transfer benchmark.

The risk of fragmented implementation — identified as a NDS1 failure — is addressed through enhanced Provincial, District, and Ward Coordinating Committees that align sub-national implementation with national priorities.

### 3.4 African Peer Review Mechanism (APRM)

In 2024, Zimbabwe established the National Institutional Framework for the APRM under the auspices of the African Union. The mechanism covers six thematic areas: democracy and political governance, economic governance and management, corporate governance, socio-economic development, and state resilience to disasters and shocks.

In 2025, Zimbabwe was peer reviewed on Economic Governance and Management — making it the first APRM assessment under the new framework. During NDS2, the APRM will provide periodic, externally validated governance assessments, creating accountability pressure and identifying areas for structured improvement.

## 4. Enterprise Risk Management in Zimbabwe's Public Sector

### 4.1 The 2023 Public Sector Risk Framework

The most significant structural development in Zimbabwe's public sector risk management is the **2023 Public Sector Risk Framework**, launched under a central directive from the Ministry of Finance, Economic Development and Investment Promotion (MoFEDIP). This framework marks a decisive break from defensive, siloed compliance thinking toward proactive, institution-wide Enterprise Risk Management.

The framework's implementation — coordinated through tender MoFEDIPCON12024 — requires MDAs to:
1. Customize internal risk policies based on the centrally-defined framework.
2. Draft formal **Risk Appetite Statements** specifying each institution's tolerance for different risk categories.
3. Align departmental **Risk Registers** with the Ministry's broader ERM principles.
4. Train senior executives in ERM principles before official adoption.

The design intent is clear: create a **standardized, nationwide risk vocabulary** that prevents institutions from operating in risk silos and directly informs capital allocation decisions.

### 4.2 Operational ERM in Practice

Evidence from the eGP procurement system shows the 2023 framework generating downstream demand across the public sector:

- **National Building Society (NBS)** procured an integrated Audit, Compliance, and Operational Risk Management System to consolidate risk portfolios and replace manual spreadsheets with automated, real-time risk reporting dashboards.
- **Risk Register Training programs** have been procured by multiple agencies to embed ERM competencies at the staff level.
- **Risk policy manuals** — high-quality printed documents — have been procured to ensure consistent communication of risk policies across all staff levels.

The move from paper-based risk registers to digital, real-time dashboards is particularly significant. It transforms risk management from a retrospective compliance exercise into a live operational intelligence function.

### 4.3 Financial and Macroeconomic Risk

NDS2 identifies macroeconomic instability as a primary development risk. Key risk management targets include:

- **Inflation**: Annual inflation targeted to decelerate from 20% (December 2025) to single-digit levels of approximately 5% by 2030, aligned with SADC macro-economic convergence targets.
- **Fiscal deficit**: Sustained below 3% of GDP to contain domestic public debt and restore fiscal space.
- **Currency stability**: Maintaining the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) currency's stability through coordinated monetary, fiscal, and exchange rate management.
- **Debt management**: The **Medium-Term Debt Management Strategy (2026–2030)** guides prudent debt management, with credit risk assessments for all new borrowing conducted through the External and Domestic Debt Management Committee. New concessional borrowing is targeted only at high-impact projects with strong repayment potential.

### 4.4 Disaster Risk and Climate Resilience

NDS2 recognizes climate change as a material sovereign risk. Increased frequency and intensity of climate-induced disasters — floods, droughts, and extreme weather — threaten agricultural output, infrastructure, and human welfare.

Risk management responses include:
- Full operationalization of the **National Emergency Operation Centre (NEOC)** with integrated early warning systems.
- Embedding **climate-risk assessments** at municipal and district levels in all infrastructure planning.
- Mandatory investment in flood control systems and early-warning mechanisms.
- Coordination with regional and international partners on disaster risk reduction.

During NDS1, Zimbabwe launched the NEOC and upgraded weather radar systems, improving forecasting capability. NDS2 builds on this by mainstreaming disaster risk reduction into development planning rather than treating it as a separate emergency response function.

### 4.5 Cybersecurity as an Enterprise Risk Priority

The Cyber and Data Protection Act (2021) established the legal architecture for cybersecurity governance. NDS2 elevates this to a governance and enterprise risk priority, requiring all public institutions to:

- Establish **dedicated cybersecurity governance structures** with clear oversight and accountability.
- Develop and approve cybersecurity frameworks and risk assessment plans.
- Monitor incident trends and emerging threats, reviewing major breaches and lessons learned.
- Integrate cybersecurity into national education and training systems.

Operationally, the **Zimbabwe Manpower Development Fund (ZIMDEF)** procured implementation of an Enterprise Governance of Information Technology System using the **COBIT 2019 framework** — a globally recognized standard for IT governance. This deployment aligns ZIMDEF's IT infrastructure with national human capital mandates while systematically addressing cybersecurity risks.

A national survey commissioned by **POTRAZ** under the Cyber and Data Protection Act assessed data privacy awareness and compliance across economic sectors, providing the evidence base for targeted remediation.

## 5. Digital GRC: Technology-Enabled Transparency

### 5.1 The Electronic Government Procurement (eGP) System

The eGP portal, managed by the Procurement Regulatory Authority of Zimbabwe (PRAZ), is the most visible manifestation of Zimbabwe's digital governance transformation. By requiring all public procurement above threshold to be conducted through the portal, the system creates a real-time, auditable record of government spending decisions.

The eGP system delivers GRC value through multiple mechanisms:
- **Transparency**: All tender notices, bid evaluations, and award decisions are publicly accessible.
- **Compliance gatekeeping**: Bidders must submit statutory documents — NSSA compliance certificates, ZIMRA tax clearances, Certificate of Incorporation, PRAZ vendor registration — as mandatory requirements. Failure to provide any single document results in immediate disqualification, regardless of technical merit.
- **Standardization**: Common evaluation criteria, including quality-weighting systems (e.g., ZERA's experience-based scoring: >10 years = 20%, 5–10 years = 10%, <5 years = 5%), create predictable, merit-based selection processes.

The principle that **administrative compliance cannot be overridden by technical competence** — illustrated by ZIDA's disqualification of a bidder for a missing NSSA certificate — is a fundamental risk control: it ensures that government engages only with legally compliant, registered, tax-paying entities.

### 5.2 Board Governance and Performance Evaluations

Public entity board evaluations have evolved from routine administrative exercises into critical governance compliance tools. Procurement patterns on the eGP system reveal a sector-wide shift toward technology-enabled, internationally benchmarked board assessments.

Key characteristics:
- Mandatory use of **ICT innovations and digital tools** in conducting board evaluations (TIMB requirement under tender TIMB/RFP/05A/2025).
- Assessment scope includes: board and committee effectiveness, terms of reference adequacy, individual director assessments, governance practices, and recommendations for improvement.
- High experience thresholds: firms with over 10 years of corporate evaluation experience receive maximum technical scores, reflecting the premium placed on professional depth.
- **NatPharm** used single-source selection for a two-year corporate governance contract, indicating sustained, relationship-based governance advisory arrangements.

### 5.3 Whistleblower Systems and Ethics Technology

Automated ethics systems are being deployed to strengthen ethical compliance in public entities. The **Tobacco Industry Marketing Board (TIMB)** procured a specialized "Tip-Off Anonymous" whistleblower platform with the following specifications:
- Guarantees absolute anonymity for whistleblowers
- Supports multiple reporting channels: text, voice, and email
- Specifically configured for reporting corruption and malpractice by TIMB officials and industry stakeholders
- 24/7 accessibility
- Monthly and ad-hoc reporting to TIMB management and the Board Audit Committee

This deployment reflects a broader trend: ethics oversight is migrating from human-administered processes (prone to retaliation risk and suppression) to platform-mediated systems with structural anonymity guarantees.

### 5.4 ESG Integration in Operational Auditing

A significant development in Zimbabwe's audit landscape is the integration of **Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG)** parameters into standard operational audits. TIMB's internal audit mandate now explicitly includes:

- Assessment of ESG practices against best practices and sustainable development standards.
- Audit of environmental compliance alongside traditional financial and operational audit dimensions.
- Benchmarking current practices against international sustainability standards.

This signals that ESG reporting is becoming a mandatory compliance requirement rather than a voluntary corporate social responsibility initiative — a structural shift with implications for how public entities manage reputational and regulatory risk.

### 5.5 Dual-Currency Reporting and Asset Valuation

Zimbabwe's dual-currency environment (USD and ZiG) creates a unique compliance dimension. Asset valuation systems must now deliver parallel financial reports in both currencies to ensure audit readiness and compliance with group reporting obligations. **Defold Mine's** procurement of external valuation services for the Kamitivi Tin Mine and Transminerals assets — with separate USD and ZiG reports for 2025 — illustrates the operational complexity this requirement imposes.

## 6. Anti-Corruption: Architecture and Implementation

### 6.1 Institutional Architecture

Zimbabwe's anti-corruption architecture is multi-layered:

- **ZACC** serves as the primary anti-corruption body, with provincial offices now extending to Mashonaland West and Mashonaland Central.
- **The National Prosecuting Authority** provides prosecutorial capacity, recording court clearance rates of 71.83% (2023) and 67.97% (2024).
- **Specialized anti-corruption courts** adjudicate corruption cases with dedicated dockets, reducing case backlog and improving deterrence.
- **The Office of the Auditor General** provides financial oversight, while Parliament exercises legislative scrutiny.

NDS2 commits to clearly defining **role clarity between key anti-corruption institutions** — recognizing that overlapping mandates and jurisdictional ambiguity have historically weakened coordinated enforcement.

### 6.2 Integrity Pledges and Culture Change

During NDS1, ZACC superintended over the signing of more than **1,100 integrity pledges** with public officials across MDAs, alongside the creation of **150 Institutional Integrity Committees**. These instruments represent a behavioral change strategy: rather than relying solely on enforcement and deterrence, they create formal commitments to ethical conduct and peer accountability mechanisms within institutions.

NDS2 will build on this foundation with:
- Expansion of anti-corruption courts
- Enactment of comprehensive whistleblower protection legislation
- Asset forfeiture and recovery provisions for illicit wealth
- Mandatory asset declaration by all public officials

### 6.3 Procurement as Anti-Corruption Infrastructure

The eGP system is, at its core, an anti-corruption infrastructure investment. By digitalizing procurement, standardizing evaluation criteria, and creating publicly auditable decision records, it systematically removes the information asymmetries and discretionary spaces that corruption exploits.

PRAZ's maintenance of an **ISO 9001:2015 Quality Management System** — including a 12-day internal audit program to identify operational non-conformities and apply root-cause analysis — reflects an institution-level commitment to continuous compliance improvement. The USD 15,650 investment in QMS maintenance is a measurable signal of institutional seriousness about governance standards.

## 7. State-Owned Enterprise Governance

### 7.1 The Mutapa Investment Fund

The **Mutapa Investment Fund** (formerly the Sovereign Wealth Fund) was created to centralize and unlock the commercial value of Zimbabwe's state-owned enterprise portfolio — over 30 SOEs spanning agro-processing, mining, fertilizer production, water treatment, veterinary chemicals, and timber.

NDS2 commits to reviewing the **Sovereign Wealth Fund of Zimbabwe Act** to:
- Enhance governance provisions ensuring robust, transparent, and accountable SOE oversight
- Delineate powers between Mutapa and individual SOEs
- Require disclosure of annual reports aligned with the **Santiago Principles** (which mandate publication of audited financial statements and operational reports)
- Introduce procurement regulations with external oversight and audit
- Mandate publication of contingent liabilities and borrowings
- Require prior Ministerial approval and Parliamentary notification for borrowings above specified thresholds

### 7.2 Performance Management and Accountability

The current SOE performance management framework will be strengthened through:
- A **comprehensive performance management framework** with explicit incentives and penalties
- A **performance-based penalty regime** including provisions for dismissal of boards and management in cases of gross underperformance
- Alignment with the **Public Entities Corporate Governance Act** and **PPDPA Act**

This reflects a recognition that governance frameworks without enforcement consequences fail to change behavior. The penalty regime — extending to board dismissal — creates meaningful accountability pressure on SOE leadership.

## 8. Key Challenges and Structural Gaps

### 8.1 Funding and Disbursement Failures

The most consistent failure mode in Zimbabwe's governance and development programs is the gap between commitments and disbursements. NDS1 was repeatedly hampered by delayed budget disbursements, inadequate allocations, and scaling down of planned activities. The constitutional 5% Inter-Governmental Fiscal Transfer commitment was not consistently met.

NDS2 acknowledges this explicitly: "funding constraints, delayed disbursements, inadequate allocations and scaling down of planned activities hampered the delivery of outcomes." Closing the commitment-disbursement gap is not merely a financial management issue — it is a governance credibility issue.

### 8.2 Inter-Agency Coordination Deficits

NDS1 suffered from weak coordination and collaboration among MDAs and local authorities, resulting in duplication of efforts, operational inefficiencies, and wasted resources. Thematic Working Groups "often lacked effective governance, as meetings were not consistently chaired at senior levels, thereby undermining decision-making authority."

NDS2's response — Cabinet-level Coordinating Committees chaired at Vice President level, Provincial/District/Ward Coordinating Committees, and a Whole-of-Government approach — addresses the structural design. The behavioral and incentive dimensions of effective coordination remain a persistent implementation risk.

### 8.3 Administrative Compliance Gaps

Evidence from GRC procurement evaluations reveals that statutory compliance — NSSA certificates, ZIMRA tax clearances, company registration documents — remains an active compliance challenge for service providers. The frequency of disqualifications for missing administrative documentation suggests that the baseline compliance culture among professional service firms is still developing.

### 8.4 Dual-Currency Complexity

Zimbabwe's dual-currency environment (USD and ZiG) introduces systemic complexity into financial reporting, asset valuation, audit, and risk management. Institutions must maintain parallel reporting capabilities, and any currency instability translates directly into valuation uncertainty and audit risk.

### 8.5 Capacity Constraints

The GRC transformation agenda is ambitious — ERM frameworks, COBIT-based IT governance, ISO certification, ESG auditing, digital whistleblower platforms — and places significant demands on institutional capacity. The emphasis on **over 10 years of experience** for governance consultants reflects market recognition that these are not commodity services. Building internal GRC capacity within MDAs, not just procuring it externally, is a medium-term imperative.

## 10. Strategic Recommendations

**1. Operationalize the IRBM system fully before NDS2 mid-term.**
The e-Whole of Government Performance Management System is the accountability infrastructure on which all NDS2 governance commitments depend. Prioritize system operationalization, MDA data unit establishment, and ZimStats integration to ensure real-time performance tracking is functional by 2027.

**2. Ring-fence devolution fund disbursements.**
Create a constitutional compliance mechanism that automatically transfers at least 5% of national revenue to sub-national tiers, removing discretionary control that has historically resulted in under-disbursement. This is both a governance requirement and a risk management measure against local-level service delivery failure.

**3. Accelerate whistle-blower legislation.**
The absence of whistle-blower protection legislation is a material gap in Zimbabwe's anti-corruption architecture. Enact legislation by 2027, drawing on operational learnings from TIMB's whistleblower platform deployment and international models from Kenya, South Africa, and the OECD framework.

**4. Mandate ERM system integration across all MDAs within three years.**
The 2023 Public Sector Risk Framework requires implementation — but adoption rates and quality vary. Establish a PRAZ-linked compliance tracker requiring all MDAs above a defined expenditure threshold to demonstrate operational ERM systems (risk registers, appetite statements, real-time dashboards) by 2028.

**5. Expand COBIT and cybersecurity governance beyond pilot deployments.**
ZIMDEF's COBIT 2019 deployment should serve as a reference implementation. Develop a rollout plan extending IT governance frameworks to all entities managing critical infrastructure, citizen data, or financial systems. The Cyber and Data Protection Act's requirement for dedicated cybersecurity governance structures needs enforcement teeth.

**6. Harmonize GRC standards across SOEs.**
Mutapa Investment Fund's oversight of 30+ SOEs creates an opportunity for standardized GRC frameworks — common risk registers, reporting templates, board evaluation criteria, and audit protocols. A Mutapa-wide GRC standards manual, aligned with the Public Entities Corporate Governance Act, would reduce heterogeneity and improve consolidated risk visibility.

**7. Publish APRM findings with implementation tracking.**
The APRM peer review on Economic Governance and Management (2025) represents externally validated evidence. Make findings, recommendations, and government responses publicly available, with annual progress reports. This converts the APRM from a periodic assessment into a continuous accountability mechanism.

**8. Invest in public sector GRC capacity, not just procurement.**
The current pattern — procuring ERM consultants, board evaluation firms, and IT governance implementers — builds external market capacity but limited internal capability. Establish a Zimbabwe Institute of Public Sector Risk Management (or equivalent) within the OPC or Ministry of Finance to develop and retain in-house GRC expertise across MDAs.

## 10. Conclusion

Zimbabwe's governance and risk management transformation is real, substantive, and accelerating — but it remains fragile and unevenly distributed across the public sector.

The structural foundations are sound: a constitutional architecture that mandates accountability, a legal framework (PPDPA, Corporate Governance Act, Cyber and Data Protection Act) that creates enforceable standards, an eGP system that operationalizes procurement transparency, and NDS2 as a coherent strategic framework elevating governance to foundational priority status.

The operational reality is more uneven. The 2023 Public Sector Risk Framework is generating genuine ERM adoption in leading institutions — the National Building Society's integrated risk management system and ZIMDEF's COBIT deployment are markers of institutional seriousness. But the gap between framework adoption and framework effectiveness remains wide in many MDAs.

The critical success factor for the 2026–2030 period is not more frameworks — Zimbabwe has frameworks. It is the conversion of frameworks into behavioral norms: board chairs who actively use risk dashboards, procurement officers who enforce compliance without exception, budget officials who disburse devolution funds on schedule, and public officials who declare assets without legal compulsion.

NDS2's Vision 2030 target — a Prosperous and Empowered Upper Middle-Income Society — is achievable only if the governance and risk management infrastructure being built today performs reliably under stress. The trajectory is positive. The work ahead is large.
